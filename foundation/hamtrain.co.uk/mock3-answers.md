# MOCK TEST 3 — ANSWERS

## Question 1 — D

"M" as the second letter of a callsign (e.g. MM0ABC) would indicate that the signal is coming from Scotland

## Question 2 — B

You are required to identify yourself by giving your callsign when you change mode.

## Question 3 — B

"General reception" means a message for anyone who might be listening. You can't transmit to listeners (that's broadcasting) - you can only talk to one amateur. The exception is when calling CQ or on an established 'net.

## Question 4 — D

The Ofcom licence states: "A person authorised by Ofcom may require the Radio Equipment, or any part thereof, to be modified or restricted in use, or temporarily or permanently closed down immediately"

## Question 5 — A

Refer to the "Foundation Licence Parameters" table on page 2 of the 4-page exam booklet and look up each frequency.

Only 7.007MHz is listed (7000-7100kHz). 10.155MHz, 21.475MHz and 24.880MHz do not fit in any of the ranges, and so cannot be used at Foundation.

## Question 6 — D

The EMF restrictions (relating to public exposure of signals) apply for power 10 Watts EIRP and above, which is equal to 6.1 Watts ERP.

## Question 7 — D

## Question 8 — D

Batteries deliver DC, not AC.

A secondary battery is rechargeable - it has a reversible chemical process. Looking for an easy way to remember the difference? A rechargeable battery can be used for a SECOND time.

## Question 9 — C

Here, we are talking about digital radios, typically using software. Accordingly, remember for the exam that a computing device is required to process digital signals.

## Question 10 — C

Single sideband is more efficient than AM (which uses 2 sidebands). From 10MHz upwards, upper sideband is normally used for single sideband operation.

## Question 11 — A

If you are distorting, you may be over-modulating or over-deviating. Reducing the transmitted audio level by adjusting the microphone gain is recommended.

## Question 12 — B

This is a receiver box diagram question. After the signal has been selected and the RF carrier removed, the weak audio signal is amplified so that it is loud enough for the speakers or headphones.

## Question 13 — A

For higher frequencies (such as VHF & UHF), the loss in the feeder is higher than for HF frequencies. To minimise loss, it's important to use a low-loss feeder, especially over a long cable run.

## Question 14 — B

A gain of 3dB doubles the power. See the conversion table on page 4 of the 4-page exam booklet - 3dB has a multiplier of 2, so 10 Watts x 2 is 20 Watts ERP (effective radiated power)

## Question 15 — B

If you get a high SWR reading, then there is a mismatch between the transmitter and antenna that can potentially damage the transmitter. You would typically resolve this using an AMU (Antenna Matching Unit)

## Question 16 — C

The Ionosphere is made up of layers of gas that allow HF signals to be 'bent' back to Earth, enabling worldwide propagation, bouncing signals around the planet.

## Question 17 — A

For the exam, remember that snow, ice and heavy rain can attenuate signals at UHF and above.

If you answered 'tropospheric ducting', that's more likely to increase the range, not to reduce it.

## Question 18 — A

Amateur radio signals are least likely to interfere with a microwave oven. Items such as a CCTV camera, wi-fi router and a computer monitor are more susceptible to RF interference

## Question 19 — C

Of the available choices, a copper stake hammered into the ground would be the most efficient RF earth. Use of the mains earth or a radiator is not recommended for safety reasons. Horizontal wires below the grass are not deep enough to make an effective RF earth.

## Question 20 — D

All three options (logging, testing and getting advice) are good steps in working to resolve any possible amateur radio interference problem.

## Question 21 — C

It is important to ask, "Is this frequency in use?" in case there are two stations already using the frequency. One may be transmitting, but too far away for you to hear

## Question 22 — A

Refer to the 2m and 20m band plans that are printed in the 4-page exam booklet! 144.500MHz is for SSTV (images), 145.725MHz is a repeater output, and 14.100MHz is a beacon. Therefore, only 14.285MHz is usable for voice.

## Question 23 — D

There are several digital voice formats that may not be compatible with each other.

If you chose "use the correct CTCSS tones", CTCSS tones are only used on analogue repeaters, not over DV.

## Question 24 — C

A fuse is a safety device that contains a wire that is designed to melt if an excessive current passes through it, thus breaking the circuit.

## Question 25 — B

Eye protection must be worn when using tools such as drills to prevent eye damage from small metal particles (known as swarf)

## Question 26 — A

A lethal electric shock can result from antennas and ladders touching or attracting arcing from overhead lines.
