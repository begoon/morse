# MOCK TEST 6 — ANSWERS

## Question 1 — D

Refer to the Schedule in the 4-page exam booklet when you get any questions about specific frequencies.

In the Schedule on page 2 under 431-432MHz, you'll see that this range cannot be used within 100km of Charing Cross in London.

## Question 2 — A

You need to give your callsign when you start transmitting on a frequency for the first time, so you would need to give your callsign on 145.500MHz as part of your CQ call, and again when you move to the new frequency.

## Question 3 — B

The use of codes and abbreviations is permitted on amateur radio. Examples include: Morse code, "CQ", "73" and Q codes (like QTH, QSL, QRT, etc) - as these are all public domain radio codes & abbreviations.

What's not permitted are SECRET codes or encryption that make the message unintelligible to other radio users.

## Question 4 — D

The licence states: "The Licensee shall permit any person authorised by Ofcom to inspect the Licence and Radio Equipment, at any and all reasonable times or, when in the opinion of that person an urgent situation exists, at any time to ensure that the Radio Equipment is being used in accordance with the terms of the Licence."

## Question 5 — B

Refer to the "Foundation Licence Parameters" table in the 4-page exam booklet. The range 431-432MHz has a restriction preventing use within a 100km radius of Charing Cross, London.

## Question 6 — A

You need to complete an assessment to demonstrate that the public is not exposed to strong EMF (electromagnetic fields), and keep a record of those assessments.

## Question 7 — B

This is an Ohm's Law question. The formula is V = I x R. From the triangle, you can work out that R = V/I. So, the resistance = 9 volts divided by 3 amps, giving the answer of 3 ohms.

## Question 8 — B

Use the conversion table in the 4-page exam booklet to calculate this. 4 metres on the left (wavelength) axis converts to roughly 70MHz on the bottom axis

## Question 9 — C

In digital radio terms, digital signals are a stream of finite values at a specific sampling interval.

By contrast, analogue signals are constantly changing in amplitude, frequency or both.

## Question 10 — B

Waveform height does not vary with the volume of the audio for Frequency Modulation.

It is AM signals that vary in amplitude (height). FM signals are of the same height, but vary in frequency.

## Question 11 — D

The RF Power Amplifier is connected to the antenna and can be damaged by power being reflected back from a mismatched antenna.

## Question 12 — D

A mathematical operation in the Digital Processor stage sifts the signals into separate frequency components. Refer to the SDR receiver block diagram in the training material.

## Question 13 — B

A long length of coaxial feeder cable is likely to exhibit 'loss'. Thicker, high-quality cable helps to reduce loss.

## Question 14 — B

10 Watts EIRP (gain relative to a theoretical antenna) is equal to 6.1 Watts ERP (gain relative to a dipole).

Why does this answer matter? Ofcom quotes EMF exposure limits as 10W EIRP in the licence, which is equivalent to 6.1W ERP, and so this is a common value amateurs need to know for antenna assessments. It is also a common exam question!

## Question 15 — D

An antenna connected to a transmitter, but used on the wrong band, could reflect some of the power back down the feeder, potentially damaging the transmitter. An AMU (Antenna Matching Unit) can be used to match the antenna impedance.

## Question 16 — B

Ionisation of the Ionosphere is caused mainly by ultraviolet radiation from the sun. This has a positive effect on HF propagation.

## Question 17 — C

Obstructions blocking the "line of sight", such as buildings, hills, etc, is the other factor affecting the range of VHF/UHF signals.

If you answered "amount of UV from the sun reaching the Ionosphere", that's for HF, not VHF/UHF signals.

## Question 18 — A

The ability of any piece of equipment to function correctly in the presence of strong RF signals is known as "immunity". A lower immunity means the equipment is more likely to be affected.

## Question 19 — B

You should ideally only use the power that you need to make a contact. If you can make contact at 1 Watt, you don't need to use 10 Watts. By using unneeded power, you could be causing interference to other people who want to use that frequency, as well as possibly causing EMC issues.

## Question 20 — D

In cases of possible interference, it would be useful to keep a log of transmissions to help identify whether or not you may be the cause of any interference.

## Question 21 — C

The exam syllabus states that, as a minimum, a log should contain: "date, time (in UTC), mode, and the call sign of station worked"

## Question 22 — D

Refer to the 2m band plan that is printed on page 4 of the 4-page exam booklet. Almost at the bottom of the 2m band plan, you'll see an entry on 145.800MHz for "FM/DV Space Communications (e.g. ISS) - Space-to-Earth"

If you chose 145.200, that is Earth-to-Space, not Space-to-Earth.

## Question 23 — B

It is common for callsigns to be set up in software on a DV radio, and for these to be automatically transmitted. If you don't change the callsign and transmit with someone else's callsign (not under supervision), you are technically in breach of the licence.

## Question 24 — C

Special care is needed with earthing arrangements, and a competent professional must be consulted prior to making any modifications.

## Question 25 — A

For safety reasons, to prevent solder or flux from splashing into the eyes, eye protection should be worn whilst soldering.

## Question 26 — D

This question relates to safety. It is not true that equipment should be earthed to copper central heating pipes. Never use heating pipes as an earth, as this is dangerous.
