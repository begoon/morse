# MOCK TEST 2 — ANSWERS

## Question 1 — A

Ofcom requires that you must confirm that the details shown on the licence remain valid at least once every five years.

## Question 2 — B

You are required to identify yourself by giving your callsign when you start transmitting on a frequency, and to give your callsign as frequently as is practicable during transmissions.

## Question 3 — A

In this question, a Full licence holder (M0ABC) visits you, a Foundation licence holder. If you are happy for them to use your radio equipment, then they can operate using their callsign under their own licence terms. Commonly, they'd append the suffix /A ("M0ABC slash A") to let people know they were at an address other than their own home address.

## Question 4 — B

The licence states: "If required by a person authorised by Ofcom, the Licensee shall keep a permanent record (a "log") of such matters concerning the operation of the Radio Equipment, over such period, and in such form, as the authorised person may require."

## Question 5 — A

Refer to the "Foundation Licence Parameters" table in the 4-page exam booklet. Look for the entry "14275-14350kHz" on page 2 (10 lines down). The "Status of Amateur Radio Satellite" column for this range says "Not allocated" for satellite use.

## Question 6 — C

EMF (electromagnetic fields) have to be within International limits regarding exposure to the general public.

If you answered "Ensuring that signals do not cause interference to nearby electrical devices", that's EMC (electromagnetic compatibility), not EMF (electromagnetic fields)

## Question 7 — A

The formula for calculating power (in Watts) is as follows: Volts x Current (P = V x I)

The triangle can help you remember this.

## Question 8 — C

As the frequency increases, the wavelength decreases.

## Question 9 — C

Analogue signals are constantly changing in amplitude, frequency or both.

By comparison, digital signals are a stream of finite values at a specific sampling interval.

## Question 10 — D

The diagram represents an AM signal - Voice and carrier signals modulated by amplitude.

## Question 11 — C

Take a look at the transmitter block diagram in the training material. The Power Amplifier is connected to the antenna. (It has nothing to do with tuning, and if you selected 'demodulator', that is in a receiver, not a transmitter)

## Question 12 — A

The definition of a radio receiver in the exam syllabus is a device designed "to recover information sent from one place to another using electromagnetic radiation".

## Question 13 — A

Coaxial cable is the common type of feeder used between the transmitter and antenna. It is unbalanced and has a single inner core, with an outer screen (usually made of braided copper, to keep the signal inside the cable)

## Question 14 — C

This is a quarter-wave vertical antenna, commonly used for VHF/UHF. Note the 4 radials at the base, and the lack of a coil (found on 5/8 wave antennas).

## Question 15 — C

An SWR of 4:1 is undesirable and should be corrected, as prolonged use at high SWR may damage your transmitter. You should reduce the SWR using an AMU (Antenna Matching Unit) to prevent the risk of damage.

## Question 16 — A

Bands "open" and "close" depending on the time of day. Long-distance communication, known as DX (distant), is all to do with solar radiation acting on the ionosphere.

It also helps to try to work a country when they are awake.

## Question 17 — C

Certain atmospheric conditions, such as 'sporadic E' and 'atmospheric ducting', can extend the range of VHF and/or UHF signals, allowing communication over longer distances than normal.

## Question 18 — D

A domestic radio receiver has an antenna and is capable of receiving radio signals, so it is more likely to be affected by signals from a nearby radio transmitter than the other listed items.

## Question 19 — B

FM is less likely to interfere, as the amplitude of the signal is constant. Here's a handy tip for the exam - remember "FM = Friendly Modulation"

## Question 20 — C

The best option here is to offer to log the times of your transmissions, for comparison with the times that your neighbour is experiencing interference, to confirm whether or not you're the problem.

## Question 21 — A

The optional suffix /P being used at the end of the callsign indicates that the station is at a temporary (/Portable) location. MM7 would be a Foundation callsign operating from Scotland.

## Question 22 — B

Refer to the "Frequency Allocation Table", which is on page 4 of the 4-page exam booklet. 158.525-160.6MHz is assigned to Land Mobile.

## Question 23 — C

Most overseas countries do not recognise the UK Foundation licence. If you want to operate overseas, you might want to work towards getting a UK 'Full' licence, which is recognised by many other countries' administrators.

## Question 24 — C

A clearly-marked 'Off' switch is recommended so that the power can easily be turned off in an emergency (such as electrocution)

## Question 25 — A

Soldering workstations must be well ventilated to avoid inhalation of solder fumes, which can cause breathing problems, particularly to asthmatics.

## Question 26 — D

This is a safety question. To avoid RF exposure/burns, you want to avoid people accidentally coming into contact with transmitting antennas and other conductors.
